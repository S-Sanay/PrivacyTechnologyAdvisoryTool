USE CASE: Population Health Analytics with Differential Privacy
==============================================================

COMPANY: NovaCare Health Systems
ROLE: A regional hospital network with 12 facilities serving ~400,000 patients
GOAL: Release aggregate analytics on patient outcomes, drug efficacy, and disease prevalence
      to pharmaceutical partners and public health researchers — without exposing individual records.

---

BACKGROUND
----------
NovaCare maintains a central data warehouse containing:
  - Patient demographics (name, DOB, SSN, address)
  - Medical history (diagnoses, ICD-10 codes, chronic conditions)
  - Prescription records (drug name, dosage, duration, prescribing physician)
  - Lab results (blood panels, biometrics, genetic markers)
  - Insurance and billing records

A pharmaceutical company, MedAxion, wants to license a report answering:

  Q1. What percentage of Type 2 diabetes patients in the 45-65 age group responded
      positively to Metformin vs. GLP-1 agonists over a 12-month period?

  Q2. What is the 90-day readmission rate for post-cardiac-event patients
      broken down by ZIP code, sex, and smoking status?

  Q3. How does hypertension prevalence correlate with income bracket
      across NovaCare's service area?

These questions are highly valuable but answering them naively (even with names stripped)
risks re-identification — especially in small subgroups (e.g., only 3 patients in a ZIP
code have a specific rare co-morbidity).

---

THREAT MODEL
------------
Without privacy protection, an adversary with auxiliary knowledge (public records,
insurance databases, social media) could:
  1. Cross-reference age + ZIP + rare diagnosis to re-identify individuals.
  2. Infer sensitive conditions (HIV status, mental health, addiction) from drug data.
  3. Reconstruct individual lab values from published aggregate statistics.

Legal exposure: HIPAA minimum necessary standard, GDPR Art. 9 (special category data),
state biometric privacy laws.

---

SOLUTION: Differential Privacy Pipeline
----------------------------------------

NovaCare implements a query-answering system with (ε, δ)-differential privacy,
where ε = 1.0 and δ = 1e-6 per query batch (calibrated to acceptable privacy loss).

Step 1 — Data Ingestion & Tokenization
  Raw records stay in the secure warehouse. All direct identifiers (name, SSN, MRN,
  full DOB) are replaced with pseudonymous tokens for internal processing. No tokens
  leave the warehouse.

Step 2 — Sensitivity Analysis per Query
  For each query, the system computes the global sensitivity Δf — the maximum
  change in the query result from adding or removing one individual's record.

  Example for Q1:
    f = (count of positive responders to Metformin) / (total Metformin patients in cohort)
    Δf = 1 / (cohort size)   [bounded sensitivity]

Step 3 — Noise Injection
  The Laplace mechanism is applied for count/proportion queries:
    noisy_result = true_result + Laplace(0, Δf / ε)

  For Q2 (multiple subgroup breakdowns), the Gaussian mechanism with composition
  accounting (via the moments accountant / Rényi DP) is used to stay within budget
  across correlated queries.

  For Q3 (correlation coefficient), a private covariance estimator clips
  individual contributions before aggregation.

Step 4 — Suppression of Small Cells
  Any subgroup with a noisy count below threshold k=10 is suppressed entirely
  and reported as "<10" to prevent the noise from being reverse-engineered via
  repeated queries.

Step 5 — Privacy Budget Tracking
  Each partner (MedAxion, public health agencies) receives an allocated ε budget.
  A privacy accountant logs every query against that budget. When budget is exhausted,
  further queries are denied until the next contract period.

Step 6 — Audit & Output Review
  All released outputs pass through a disclosure review board that checks:
    - No cell counts below suppression threshold
    - No combination of released statistics that allows differencing attacks
    - ε spent vs. allocated ratio
  The final report is signed with a cryptographic audit log entry.

---

EXAMPLE QUERY FLOW
------------------

MedAxion submits Q1:
  "Metformin vs GLP-1 response rate, age 45-65, Type 2 diabetes, 2023-2024"

Internal true counts (never released):
  Metformin positive response:  1,847 / 3,102  = 59.5%
  GLP-1 positive response:      2,011 / 2,876  = 69.9%

Sensitivity: Δf ≈ 1/2,900 (roughly, per cohort)
Noise scale: Laplace(0, 1/2900 / 1.0) → standard deviation ≈ 0.00049

Noisy released values:
  Metformin: 59.5% ± ~0.05%   → released as 59.5%
  GLP-1:     69.9% ± ~0.05%   → released as 69.9%

Privacy cost: ε = 0.08 (charged to MedAxion's annual budget of ε = 2.0)

The noise is imperceptible at this cohort size, yet the mathematical guarantee means
no individual patient's record could have meaningfully changed the published result —
their participation is protected.

---

OUTCOME
-------
NovaCare successfully monetizes population health insights, MedAxion gets statistically
valid efficacy data for regulatory submissions, and no individual patient's medical
history is exposed or re-identifiable. The differential privacy guarantee is
mathematically provable, not policy-dependent — it holds even if the recipient is
actively adversarial.

Privacy parameters published in the report's methodology section:
  ε = 1.0 (per query), δ = 1e-6, mechanism = Laplace/Gaussian (per query type),
  composition = Rényi DP moments accountant, suppression threshold k = 10.

QUIZ ANSWERS (privacy-advisor tool)
-------------------------------------
These are the answers that correctly characterize this use case in the advisor quiz.
The expected recommendation is: Differential Privacy (Central DP, ε = 0.5).

Q1.  What is your organization's primary privacy objective?
     → "Run private queries or analyses on sensitive data"
       NovaCare answers statistical questions (efficacy rates, readmission rates,
       correlations) with formal provable guarantees — the defining DP use case.

Q2.  Who poses the greatest privacy risk in your scenario?
     → "External adversaries — hackers, data thieves, or unauthorized third parties"
       The threat model is re-identification by adversaries with auxiliary knowledge
       (public records, insurance databases). DP's guarantee holds against any
       external adversary regardless of background knowledge.

Q3.  Where does the sensitive data currently reside?
     → "All centralized within our organization"
       NovaCare maintains a single central data warehouse. All PHI stays there;
       only noisy aggregate answers leave it.

Q4.  How many parties are involved in the computation or data access?
     → "Just our organization"
       NovaCare is the sole data holder and the only party running analyses.
       Partners (MedAxion) receive outputs, not data access.

Q5.  What is the trust model between the parties involved?
     → "A single trusted curator or our organization controls all data"
       NovaCare acts as the trusted central curator — the standard Central DP model,
       which yields far higher accuracy than Local DP for the same ε.

Q6.  What type of output does your use case require?
     → "Aggregate statistics, summaries, or query results"
       Released outputs are percentages, rates, and correlations — not raw records
       or ML model artifacts. Private aggregate statistics are DP's core output.

Q7.  How critical is exact numerical accuracy in your outputs?
     → "Small statistical noise is acceptable for stronger privacy"
       At cohort sizes of thousands, the Laplace noise added (σ ≈ 0.05%) is
       imperceptible. MedAxion can use results for regulatory submissions.

Q8.  How sensitive is the data your organization is working with?
     → "Highly sensitive — medical, financial, or legal records"
       The warehouse contains PHI: diagnoses, prescriptions, lab results, and
       insurance records. HIPAA and GDPR Art. 9 apply directly.

Q9.  Which regulatory or legal frameworks apply to your data?
     → "HIPAA (US Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)"
       NovaCare processes Protected Health Information in the US. HIPAA's
       Expert Determination de-identification path accepts DP's formal proof.

Q10. What computational resources can you dedicate to the privacy system?
     → "Moderate — some overhead is acceptable for meaningful privacy benefits"
       The DP pipeline (sensitivity analysis, noise injection, budget accounting,
       audit logging) adds meaningful but manageable infrastructure overhead.

Q11. What specifically needs to be protected in your scenario?
     → "Both individual identity and sensitive attribute values"
       Two-sided risk: re-identification via quasi-identifiers (age, ZIP, diagnosis)
       AND inference of sensitive attributes (drug response, HIV status, addiction).

Q12. Does your use case involve training or deploying machine learning models?
     → "No — this is not an ML or AI use case"
       NovaCare releases statistical analytics, not ML model artifacts. No
       gradient-based training pipeline is involved.

Q13. What is the primary format of your sensitive data?
     → "Tabular / structured data (rows and columns)"
       Medical records are stored in relational database tables with well-defined
       fields (patient ID, ICD-10 code, drug name, dosage, date, etc.).

Q14. How important is a formal mathematical privacy guarantee?
     → "Important — we want provable guarantees with practical tradeoffs"
       The (ε, δ) guarantee must be auditable for HIPAA Expert Determination and
       publishable in the methodology section — but utility tradeoffs are accepted.
